Risk of Direct Sourcing — Supplier Network & Bargaining Power

How order volume and supplier relationships affect cost, access, and production priority

✕ Risks of Going Direct
Weak Bargaining Power at ~5,000 Units/SKU
When clients source directly, their order volume may be too small to attract priority from manufacturers. The assumed order quantity of ~5,000 units per SKU may fall below the preferred MOQ of many export-oriented Vietnamese factories — especially for customized products, multiple colorways, or complex materials. Vietnamese apparel factories commonly require 300–3,000 units per style, while larger export factories prefer 3,000–10,000 units to maintain efficiency.
Production Inefficiency for Small Orders
Factories prioritize larger buyers because small orders create higher operational inefficiencies. Production lines require setup time, machine calibration, labor allocation, material sourcing, and quality inspections regardless of order size — meaning low-volume orders generate lower margins and may interrupt larger production schedules.
Direct Sourcing Clients May Experience
  • Higher unit prices due to lower economies of scale
  • MOQ surcharges for small production runs
  • Limited willingness from factories to accept orders
  • Lower production priority during peak seasons
  • Reduced flexibility for design changes
  • Difficulty accessing top-tier factories that prioritize strategic customers
  • Inconsistent quality if factories subcontract smaller orders to secondary workshops
Elevated Risk During Capacity Pressure
This risk intensifies during high manufacturing demand. Vietnam's manufacturing sector has recently experienced strong export growth and capacity pressure as more international brands diversify sourcing away from China, meaning factories increasingly prioritize long-term, high-volume customers.

Risk of Direct Sourcing — Lead Time & Speed-to-Market Risk

How fragmented direct sourcing creates delivery delays and seasonal revenue loss

✕ Risks of Going Direct
Coordination Complexity Across Markets
Direct sourcing requires clients to independently coordinate production, raw materials, suppliers, and logistics across multiple factories and countries. Research in global supply chain management shows that longer and more fragmented supply chains increase coordination complexity and operational risk. Based on case assumptions, product development requires ~30–45 days, with shipping lead times of ~14 days (Vietnam), ~16 days (Indonesia), and ~24 days (Bangladesh).
Chain-Reaction Delay Risk
Because sourcing activities are spread across different countries and suppliers, delays in one stage may create a chain reaction throughout the supply chain. Direct clients may face communication delays between suppliers, poor synchronization of production schedules, delays in fabric or raw material sourcing, shipment rescheduling, customs or export documentation issues, and missed export timelines.
Seasonal & Revenue Impact
In the fashion industry, products are highly seasonal and time-sensitive. Speed-to-market is a major competitive factor because late product arrivals result in missed seasonal demand and inventory markdowns. Late delivery leads to missed seasonal sales opportunities, increased markdowns, excess inventory accumulation, reduced responsiveness to fashion trends, and loss of revenue and market competitiveness.
2a. Strategic Relationship Risk
Factories are more responsive to long-term strategic partners with stable order flows. Smaller direct-sourcing clients may face lower responsiveness when urgent production changes, shipment adjustments, or expedited requests are required. Strong supplier relationships are critical for production prioritization, rapid problem-solving, flexible scheduling adjustments, and collaborative response during disruptions — advantages that direct clients typically lack.
2b. AI & Data Capability Risk
Direct sourcing limits supply chain visibility and predictive decision-making because clients lack integrated operational data. Fragmented supplier management reduces visibility into production progress, shipment status, supplier performance trends, lead-time variability, and disruption risks. Without centralized data infrastructure, clients struggle to implement AI-enabled tools for predictive delay forecasting, production optimization, and real-time monitoring. This is compounded by the fact that ~70% of supply chain stakeholders are not digital-native users.

Network Flexibility for Disruption Response
Li & Fung's extensive supplier network across multiple sourcing markets provides the flexibility to shift production when delays or disruptions occur in one country. This built-in redundancy ensures that no single-country delay cascades into a full delivery failure for the client.
Improved Visibility & Faster Decision-Making

The company focuses on supply chain coordination, speed, and flexibility to improve customer responsiveness and shorten lead times.

How disintermediation exposes clients to regulatory, legal, and brand reputation risk

✕ Risks of Going Direct
Compliance Complexity & Administrative Burden
While customers may nominate suppliers, bypassing LF means the client must independently manage supplier audit follow-ups, documentation collection, and social/labor standards monitoring — requiring specialized personnel and cross-country knowledge.
Limited Internal Capability & Digital Readiness
With approximately 70% of industry stakeholders not digital-natives, it is extremely difficult for a direct-sourcing client to implement digital audit systems or documentation tracking, often leading to inconsistent factory reporting and missing documents.
Regulatory & Trade Exposure
Sourcing across Greater ASEAN involves complex rules-of-origin and tariff regulations. Miscalculations by a direct-sourcing client can result in heavy financial fines or shipment seizures at customs.
ESG & Brand Reputation Risk
In the Supply Chain of the Future, transparency is non-negotiable. Bypassing LF creates a transparency loss where the client can no longer verify carbon reduction or worker wellbeing data — increasing the risk of PR scandals or market bans, particularly in the EU.
